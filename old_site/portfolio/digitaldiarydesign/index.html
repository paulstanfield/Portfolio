New Website Wall

This idea stems from my experience in trying to get traffic to a newly created website. It can be hard for people without the correct contacts or resources to get their new website/company/organisation any exposure. The Website Wall would provide people with the opportunity to upload a picture of their new website with a link and some information to the 'Website Wall', where it would then be rated by a like/dislike method in order for it to rank higher or lower on the wall. This would allow users to view The Website Wall to find out whats new on the internet and what people are currently enjoying (kind of like Digg/Stumpleupon only instead of for articles and stories this would specifically be for new websites). The Wall could be further broken down into categories such as categories for new websites relating to Business, Education or Sport. A comment section could also be added allowing users to post their opinions on these websites. This would hopefully lead to a place on the web were people are able to promote their new websites at a cost effective level whilst still retaining high levels of exposure for their site.

Responsive Resources

After reading Ethan Marcotte's Responsive Web Design book I was interested to see what other resources where out there online to help me make my creations responsive.

One of the first tasks for DES511 was to create our DES511 Diary, this would be our space on the web to upload our DES511 work and creative process. I wanted to make my diary as clean and simple as possible in order for the content to take the primary focus of the viewing however I always wanted it to being visually pleasing. Another element I wanted to incorporate into my diary was making it responsive, it had been pointed out by the lecturers in week 1 that alot of potential employers look through the final years work and diaries, having this in mind I wanted potential employers to gain access to my diary content no matter what device they were on. So I decided to look into some responsive frameworks/boilerplates through which I could build my design upon.

I found a number of useful resources online that I could use as a basis on which to build a responsive website, this would not only help me get up and running with learning the basics of responsive design but would also aid me in improving my knowledge of this area as looking into how the boilerplate is coded can only aid me in the future when I am coding other responsive websites. Some of the more useful responsive resources I researched into can be seen below.

Skeleton

Skeleton is a responsive boilerplate that helps you quickly build websites for any device size. I liked this resource as the CSS files were simple and easy to understand and manipulate to your needs. I also like that the boilerplate was the bare minimum allowing you to design your own website and build it to your requirements without the boilerplate acting as a restriction to what you wanted to create. Skeleton was also great because it worked across a number of different platforms such as Drupal and Wordpress.

Bones Boilerplate

Bones is a Wordpress boilerplate theme allowing responsive, mobile-first development. What I liked about this resource was that it was stripped down to the bare bones (no pun intended) this lead to a minimalistic and easy to understand set of code. Although this resource is designed for use in a Wordpress which somewhat limits its use if Wordpress is not the way you are looking to go, it is still a valuable tool to know about for future projects, perhaps even when it comes round to building my major project website.

320 and Up

320 and Up is another responsive web design boilerplate created by Andy Clarke. 320 and Up is a useful resource to consider due to its easy to use and lightweight approach meaning getting to grips with its code layout is a seamless task. It comes with all the things you'd expect in a responsive boilerplate; media queries, different style sheets, bootstrap styles for elements such as buttons and is written in the latest web standard of HTML5.

Twitter Bootstrap

Twitter Bootstrap is a resource I have heard quite a few fellow designers talk about and recommend. I actually implemented twitter bootstrap into a DES Client Project in Year 2, and it worked really well. Twitter bootstrap is a front end toolkit which allows rapid development of web applications. Although the bootstrap isn't responsive by default, responsive features are easily accessed by including the appropriate meta tags and stylesheets.

The homework for this week was to take one of my possible ideas, preferably the one that was the strongest and had the most legs and develop this idea further. Possible Idea Conveyed in 50 Words

Fitness Northern Ireland (working title) - Website aimed at allowing people to find ways of keeping fit in Northern Ireland (possibly refine this to just Belfast). Users would be able to search for exercise classes/team sports based on their interests and location. Other features could include; top exercise choice of the month, public reviews on various classes/team sports.

Target Audience

The target audience for this idea would be anyone with an interest in trying to keep fit, looking to get fit, wanting to get more active or who is interested in being more social in a exercise related environment. The website would not just be aimed at people who already into sport but for anyone who has an interest in taking up a form of exercise or sport be that in a serious capacity or purely as a form of relaxation and enjoyment. The audience for this website I feel has greatly increased in recent months due to the success and publicity of the London Olympics which has got people more excited about sport and keeping fit. As a result of the wide spectrum of people this could be targeted at the website will need to be tailored to fit people of varying characteristics instead of appealing to a certain type of person.

User Journeys / Requirements

The website will be simple and easy to use, the home page of the site will have the search bar which will perform the main feature and purpose of the website. There will also be pages dedicated to top exercise option of the month and a submit section which will allow people to email the website about new exercise classes/sports teams which are not currently on the website.

Searching For A Class / Sport

The website will allow the user to enter in keywords in order to search for an exercise class/team sport based on their interest and location. For example, a user could enter the words 'Circuit Training' into the search box and then there postcode into the location box which would then bring up a list of possible ways to get involved in Circuit Training in their local area. This search can be tailored according to the users specific interest and location.

Comments / Review Section

On searching for your form of exercise it will bring up a list of options related to your search. Clicking on an option will lead to a page of information, giving short but important details on the option such as name, address, brief description. This section will also have the ability to leave comments on the specific form of exercise class/team sport through using your Facebook account.

Choice Of The Month Feature

The website could also have top exercise option of the month feature, which could be tailored to people who have come to the website looking to get active but aren't entirely sure what form of exercise they are looking to get into. This top exercise option of the month feature could be created by monitoring the option which gets the most views on the website or most favourable comments in a monthly period.

Submit Section

It was highlighted in pitching this idea in Week 1 that gathering information for this project may be quite an undertaking and difficult. One way I thought I could tackle this issue is by have a submit section on the website which would allow people to send in exercise classes/sports teams which are not currently on the website. This could then be reviewed by the project team and then added to the database of the website if deemed appropriate. This would not only help gather information for the website but would also help local excercise classes, sports teams, gyms etc promote their services. Win win.

Possible Technologies Used
  • XHTML/CSS - Building the site to make it it standards complaint and contemporary in design.
  • PHP/My SQL - Databases will need to be used to store the information on the various classes/sports etc that will be called upon using the search option of the website.
  • Google Maps API - Used in order to find the users location, could also be used to find classes/sports as a result of using the information already stored on google maps. This could greatly help with data collection for this project. More research needed into this.
  • Facebook Integration - Facebook integration for the comments feature of the website.
  • Responsive Design - Designed to work on a number of different devices of different sizes in order to increase the usability of the website.

Reading: Content Strategy For The Web By Kristina Halvorson

The book taught me that content should not be put in for the sake of having content or to 'beef' up the site, but should be put in for a relevant reason and only if it is of a benefit to the project you are working on. The book also taught me that a logical hierarchy to navigate the content is also important, as it is all well and good me as the designer knowing where content is and what elements of the content are most important but this must also be conveyed to the viewer of the website who has played no part in the design and development of the site.

I found the following questions that were highlighted in the book in terms of content strategy particularly intriguing and helpful.

  • What content do we need to create? Why?
  • How will the content be structured?
  • How will users find the content?
  • How will we get from here to launch?
  • Whats next once the content is 'out there'?

These questions I thought formed the back bone of how I would create content in an effective and professional manner. I will be asking myself these questions in my current and future projects in order to keep my content strategy for the web to a high standard.

The Contract Killer By Andy Clarke

Contract Killer is a open-source contract for web designers and developers. Andy Clarke talks about the importance of contracts to protect you as a designer and your client and importance of understanding the project you are getting into and what you are signing up to complete. This was also talked about by Chris during our lecture when he stressed the importance of documenting your dialogue with clients in order to safe guard yourself when it comes to deadlines and payments.

Andy Clarke covers what you should include when writing up your own killer contract, highlighting the importance of paying attention to such things as;

  • A simple overview of who is hiring who, what they are being hired to do, when and for how much
  • What both parties agree to do and what their respective responsibilities are
  • The specifics of the deal and what is or isnt included in the scope
  • What happens when people change their minds (as they almost always do)
  • A simple overview of liabilities and other legal matters

Andy also kindly offers his own template killer contract that can be adapted to your needs whilst also going over aspects of contract creation for clients such as setting a tone and laying foundations for agreement, the fine print and payment schedules.
